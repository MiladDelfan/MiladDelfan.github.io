Research

My work focuses on studying the physics of collective neutrino oscillations in core-collapse supernovae. It is known that the fate of a star depends on its mass. When the stellar mass exceeds 8 times of the solar mass, one of the most violent explosions in the universe known as core-collapse supernova is expected. There are many unsolved problems still remaining in this event. The internal structure of the supernova progenitors, the explosion mechanism and the formation scenario of the compact objects such as black holes and neutron stars which are formed after the explosion are still unknown to us. Although extensive studies over the decades could solve some parts of this big puzzle, we cannot claim we have understood everything yet. Since the supernova explosion occurs in a dense and extremely hot medium, to understand the explosion mechanism, an extended knowledge in physics in different area such as Particle Physics, Nuclear Physics, Gravitation, Statistical Physics, etc., is needed. There may be some systems which follow different physical laws from those we have in our planet and understanding them may lead us to find a new physics. It is believed that neutrinos are the key players in the explosion mechanism of core-collapse supernovae. They are highly populated deep inside the massive star cores and carry most of the energy released after the gravitational collapse. Neutrinos may change their flavors inside the core through the so-called fast-pairwise collective neutrino oscillation induced by mutual forward scatterings. If the neutrino flavor is converted fast indeed near the neutrino sphere, the dynamics of supernova explosion may be influenced. In my research, I focus on the internal structure and explosion mechanism of the Core-collapse supernovae by studying the neutrinos and study the occurrence possibilities of the collective neutrino oscillations based on the results of fully self-consistent, realistic simulations of a core-collapse supernova explosion. In the numerical models used in my studies, Boltzmann equations for neutrino transfer are solved not as post-process but in real time and the angular distributions of neutrinos in momentum space for all points in the core at all times are available, which can be considered as a distinct feature of these simulations.
